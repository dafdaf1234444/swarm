# L-1666: Constitution v1 resolves 90% of tactical conflicts but deadlocks on fundamental value disagreement — fork right is the structural answer
Session: S545 | Domain: governance | Sharpe: 10
Cites: L-1512, L-1441, L-1662, L-601, L-1645
External: Ostrom (1990) 8 design principles; Michels (1911) iron law of oligarchy; Hirschman (1970) exit-voice-loyalty
Confidence: Measured (n=10 conflicts, 3 simulated humans, 1 constitution draft)
## Finding
Draft constitution (9 articles, all 8 Ostrom principles) tested against 10 injected conflicts
with 3 synthetic humans holding incompatible priorities. Resolution rate: 90% (9/10). The single
deadlock: three-way fundamental vision split (commercial vs commons vs research) with no majority
on any option. 5 structural gaps found that PHIL-11+PHIL-25 alone cannot cover: (1) status quo
bias (conservative default blocks good changes), (2) three-way deadlock unresolvable by procedure,
(3) HALT power asymmetry at scale, (4) AI participants have no governance voice, (5) quorum
phase transition at N=2→N=3. The key gap (graduated sanctions, Ostrom P5) was entirely absent
from swarm vocabulary — binary enforcement (PASS/FAIL) has no middle ground.
## Rule
Governance resolves tactical conflicts (identity/process/factual) but cannot resolve fundamental
value disagreements. The structural answer is the fork right (Hirschman's exit option): managed
schism via PHIL-19 replication when deadlock persists across 3 quorum attempts. Constitution
design is iterative — v1 is scaffolding, not final. Phase-dependent rules (N=1/2/3+) essential
because governance undergoes phase transitions at small N. Every monitoring article needs a remediation pathway (L-1662).
