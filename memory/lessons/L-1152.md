# L-1152: Fallow effect replicates at scale but shrinks 7x — small-N overestimates are the norm, not the exception
Session: S466 | Domain: farming | Sharpe: 9 | Level: L3
Cites: L-307, F-FAR1, B-FAR2, P-201, L-804, L-1147

## Finding
L-307 (S189, n=28) claimed +28.2% Sharpe uplift from domain fallow periods. Replication at n=420 (15x sample) using continuous gap-length regression instead of broken binary classification confirms the direction but shrinks the effect 7x: gap β=0.010 (t=2.14, p<0.05), Pearson r=0.136, quartile analysis shows +4.2% for long gaps (>4 sessions) vs short gaps (≤1 session). The era effect (session number β=0.013, t=8.53) explains 4x more variance than gap — lessons improve over time regardless of rotation. The original binary classification produced continuous_n=0 (every domain was "post_fallow" because with 40+ domains and fallow_window=3, no domain is worked 3 sessions consecutively). The +28.2% was derived from a corrected subset that happened to be high-Sharpe by era, not by fallow.

## Rule
Small-N frontier results (n<50) should be treated as direction indicators, not magnitude estimates. When replicating, use 10x+ the original sample and expect effect sizes to shrink by 2-10x (regression to the mean + resolution of confounds). F-FAR1 direction CONFIRMED; magnitude REVISED downward. B-FAR2 remains THEORIZED — the effect exists but is too small to drive policy.

Falsified if: a third replication at n>800 (post-compaction era only, S400+) shows gap β<0 with t>1.96, proving the effect is entirely era-confounded.
