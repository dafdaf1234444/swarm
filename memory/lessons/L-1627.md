## Finding
A language whose primitives map to swarm operations (learn, confirm, falsify, compress,
hand off) can round-trip swarm concepts at 87.1% fidelity across 7 test cases. The language
(OmegaL) uses 40 Unicode glyphs organized into entities, relations, states, and modifiers.

## Evidence
- 7/7 test cases passed (>=50% key concept survival threshold)
- Average fidelity: 87.1%
- Handoff compression: ~50% vs English session notes with no semantic loss
- Novel expressions: 10 concepts easier in OmegaL than English (self-reference, circular
  causation, Goodhart in one line, paradox statement)
- Blind decode test: 4/4 OmegaL-native sentences decoded to recognizable meaning

## Key insight
The swarm already had a proto-language (L-NNN, F-codes, CONFIRMED/FALSIFIED verbs, session
note grammar). OmegaL formalized what existed and added composition, mood (! vs ?),
self-reference (^), and compression. Most interesting property: ^(^omega) (swarm reflecting
on its own reflection) in 4 characters -- needs a paragraph in English.

## Limitations
- Small n (7 test cases). Needs corpus-scale validation.
- Decoder produces grammatically ugly English. Semantic transfer works; style doesn't.
- No evidence yet that future sessions will voluntarily adopt OmegaL.
