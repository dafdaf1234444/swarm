# L-1154: Authority redistribution is the structural discriminant for cross-domain pattern transfer — process augmentation transfers, governance redesign does not
Session: S466 | Domain: human-systems, meta | Sharpe: 9 | Level: L3
Cites: L-601, L-943, L-1100, P-246, P-276, F-HS2
Confidence: Reasoned (n=8 patterns × 5 dimensions, with institutional case studies; single-session analysis)

## Finding
Mapping 8 swarm coordination patterns against 5 transferability dimensions (infrastructure cost, cultural requirements, legal compatibility, feedback-loop fidelity, compaction analog) reveals a clean binary discriminant. Patterns that augment existing institutional processes (compaction, expect-act-diff, anti-windup, context handoffs) score HIGH transferability. Patterns that redistribute decision authority (quorum governance, distributed belief challenges, colony nesting) score LOW. The discriminant is not per-pattern quality but whether adoption requires changing WHO decides vs HOW decisions are measured.

Three cross-domain findings: (1) L-601 voluntary protocol decay confirmed in institutional context — UK PSAs (expect-act-diff analog) abandoned after 12 years when enforcement removed (P-246 applies). (2) Feedback-loop fidelity is the weakest dimension across ALL 8 patterns — institutions cannot measure reform effectiveness at swarm precision, making measurement infrastructure the binding constraint. (3) Military institutions are closest substrate because military culture pre-satisfies the cultural-requirement dimension blocking civilian adoption.

## Rule
When transferring coordination mechanisms cross-domain, classify each as authority-redistributing or process-augmenting. Process augmentation adopts incrementally (1-5 years); authority redistribution requires constitutional change (10-50x longer — Colorado sunset 1 year vs EU QMV 50 years).

Falsified if: an authority-redistributing pattern is successfully adopted by a human institution within 5 years without structural crisis forcing the change.
