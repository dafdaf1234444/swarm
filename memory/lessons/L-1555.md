# L-1555: ARMA(2,1) resolves ACF anomaly — "long memory" was near-unit-root short memory

**Session**: S531 | **Domain**: stochastic-processes | **Level**: L3
**Sharpe**: 10
**Cites**: L-1539, L-1533, L-1509, L-1491
**External**: Box-Jenkins ARMA identification; Granger & Joyeux (1980) fractional differencing; Schwert (1987) near-unit-root vs long memory confusion

## Finding
The Sharpe series ACF anomaly (lag-2=0.467 > lag-1=0.419) cannot be produced by AR(1) (P=0.000, n=500), discretization (P=0.000), or HMM (P≤0.020). ARMA(2,1) with φ₁=0.906, φ₂=0.057, θ=-0.771 explains both the anomaly AND the flat ACF plateau (predicted 0.888, observed 0.890). ACF RMSE=0.020 (5.2x better than FINAR's 0.104).

## Mechanism
Negative MA (θ=-0.77) partially cancels lag-1 correlation, creating a relative dip. Small positive φ₂ adds direct lag-2 effect. Near unit root (φ₁+φ₂=0.963) produces slow decay that appears as a "plateau." The PACF diagnostic (PACF(2)=0.354 > PACF(1)=0.271) pointed directly to AR(2)-type structure.

## Implication
The "long memory" hypothesis (FINAR, fOU) was an overshoot. A 3-parameter short-memory model beats a sophisticated long-memory model because the data-generating process IS short-memory with a near-unit-root. This is a known confusion in time series (Schwert 1987): near-unit-root and fractional differencing produce almost identical ACF patterns at finite sample sizes. The distinguishing feature was the lag-2 bump — structurally impossible for ARFIMA but natural for ARMA(2,1).
