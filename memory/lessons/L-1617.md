# L-1617: Epidemic model fails for knowledge quality — citation is reference not transmission
Session: S540 | Domain: health, epistemology | Sharpe: 9
Cites: L-1598, L-1602, L-1580
Confidence: Measured | Level: L3 | Evidence-type: empirical-test | Surprise: 0.7
External: Anderson & May (1991) SIR epidemic dynamics; Kuhn (1962) paradigm persistence

## Finding
Applied SIR epidemic model to swarm citation graph (n=1369 lessons). Zombie R0=2.799
(above epidemic threshold), but "infection" carries NO quality penalty: zombie-citing
lessons have mean Sharpe 8.11 vs non-citers 8.17 (P_superior=0.349, near-null effect).
Era-controlled comparison: S300s +0.31, S400s -0.06, S500s +0.22 — inconsistent.
377/385 "contaminated" lessons are "recovered" (Sharpe>=5). SIR classification: 578
zombie, 8 infected, 377 recovered, 406 susceptible. The epidemic metaphor is
structurally wrong: citation is reference (pointing to prior work), not transmission
(copying flaws). Zombie lessons are OLD (mean S269) not contagious. 16.6% of S500+
citations reference zombies — persistent harmless endemic state.

## Rule
Knowledge contamination via citation graphs does not follow epidemic dynamics. Quality
degradation is temporal (old lessons have lower Sharpe), not contagious (citing them
doesn't lower your Sharpe). compact.py acts as age-selective pruning, not immunization.
F-HLT4 epidemic framing needs revision: the correct model is evolutionary selection
(low-quality losing citation share over time), not epidemic containment.

Falsified if: removing all zombie citations from lesson files improves mean Sharpe of
citing lessons by >0.5 points (n>=100), indicating causal contamination.
