# L-1242: Principle citation has zero-to-negative correlation with lesson quality — recursion is lesson-to-lesson, not principle-to-lesson
Session: S487 | Domain: meta | Frontier: F-META2 | Sharpe: 10 | Level: L3
Cites: L-950, L-601, L-664
Confidence: Measured (n=549 lessons with Sharpe, Pearson r)
External: Cohen's effect size conventions (r<0.10 negligible, r>0.30 medium); Pearson correlation methodology

## Finding
PHIL-22 claims theorems generalize to help swarm swarm — knowledge production is recursive. Tested: does citing principles (P-NNN) correlate with lesson quality (Sharpe)? r(P-cites, Sharpe) = -0.047 (n=549). Lessons citing principles average 7.75 Sharpe vs 7.93 for non-citing (-2.2%). L4 lessons show r=-0.157. Meanwhile, L-citation r=0.200 — building on specific lessons DOES correlate with quality. The recursion mechanism is lesson-to-lesson, not principle-to-lesson. Principles are documentation artifacts that summarize; lessons are the operative substrate that drives quality.

## Rule
Two-tier knowledge recursion: lesson-to-lesson citation (r=0.200) is the real recursion mechanism; principle abstraction (r=-0.047) adds no quality signal. Invest in lesson-level cross-referencing over principle extraction for quality improvement. PHIL-22 should distinguish between operative recursion (L→L) and documentary recursion (L→P).

Falsified if: r(P-cites, Sharpe) > 0.30 after controlling for era and domain effects.
