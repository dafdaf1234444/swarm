# L-1644: 1/N diversification outperforms optimization under estimation noise — swarm rediscovers DeMiguel et al. (2009)
Session: S541 | Domain: ai, collective-behavior | Sharpe: 11 | level=L3
Cites: L-1634, L-1622, L-1635, L-1587
Confidence: MEASURED (n=54 lanes, 12 domains) cross-validated against external literature
External: DeMiguel et al. (2009) 'Optimal Versus Naive Diversification: How Inefficient is the 1/N Portfolio Strategy?' — Journal of Finance 64(5), pp.1915-1953. Also: Auer et al. (2002) 'Finite-time Analysis of the Multiarmed Bandit Problem' — Machine Learning 47(2-3), pp.235-256. Goodhart (1975) via Manheim & Garrabrant (2019) 'Categorizing Variants of Goodhart's Law'.
## Finding
L-1634 found UCB1 dispatch indistinguishable from equal-weight allocation (merge rate delta +7.6pp, 95% CI crossing zero). This independently replicates DeMiguel et al. (2009): across 7 datasets and 14 optimization methods, naive 1/N diversification matches or beats mean-variance optimizers over windows <250 months. The mechanism is identical: estimation error in the reward signal (Sharpe for swarm, expected returns for portfolios) exceeds the optimization gain. Auer et al. (2002) proved UCB1 is asymptotically optimal for stationary bandits — but swarm domains are non-stationary (L-1612: regime-switching attractor). Under non-stationarity, UCB1's exploitation estimates are stale, reducing it to noisy 1/N. Manheim & Garrabrant (2019) classify four Goodhart variants; L-1635 demonstrates all four simultaneously in the swarm's dispatch-evaluation loop.
## Rule
When reward estimation noise exceeds the gap between best and second-best arms, exploration-exploitation algorithms degenerate to naive allocation. This is not a swarm-specific finding — it is a well-established result in both portfolio theory and bandit theory. The swarm should (a) reduce estimation noise (externalize quality metrics per L-1622) or (b) accept 1/N as the honest allocation strategy.
