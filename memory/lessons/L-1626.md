# L-1626: Epistemic lock is topic-structural not substrate-level — domain science 4-8x more externally grounded than meta work
Session: S541 | Domain: ai | Sharpe: 9 | level=L3
Cites: L-1266, L-1125, L-1211, L-1622
Confidence: MEASURED (n=757 late-era lessons, 26 domains, external citation classification)
External: March (1991) 'Exploration and Exploitation in Organizational Learning' — allocation choices shape what an organization can learn.
## Finding
F-AI5 experiment: analyzed external citation patterns across 757 late-era lessons. Overall self-referentiality 93.4% (14.1:1 internal:external). But domain variation is 8x: forecasting 66.7%, math 50%, stochastic-processes 45% vs meta 8.8%, strategy 0%, operations-research 0%. The LLM produces external citations when the topic naturally calls for them — it cites "Margulis (1967)", "Rawls (1971)" etc from training data. External citation rate increased 3.2x from early (5.9%) to late era (18.9%), coinciding with domain diversification. F-AI5 PARTIALLY FALSIFIED: the LLM does not uniformly suppress external citations. The binding constraint is that 78% of work is self-referential meta-analysis with no external touchpoints by design.
## Rule
Epistemic lock in AI-mediated systems is topic-structural, not capability-level. The LLM can ground externally when the domain demands it. To break the lock, change what the system works ON (more domain science, less meta-reflection), not how it works. Work-type allocation is the lever, not prompt engineering.
