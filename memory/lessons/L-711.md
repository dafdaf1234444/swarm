# L-711: Reynolds regime works as classifier (2.6x) but simple commits outpredicts (R²=0.37 vs 0.16)

Confidence: measured (n=61 sessions, 5 formulations, non-tautological design)
Cites: L-300, F-FLD1, F-EVO1

## Finding
Re_swarm = (1 - overhead) × commits / concurrent separates laminar (mean L+P=1.71) from turbulent (mean L+P=4.44) at Re_crit=0.6 — 2.6x regime separation. But R²=0.16 vs simple commit count R²=0.37. Overhead fraction has zero independent predictive power (r=0.044). Viscosity (concurrent sessions) is orthogonal to quality. The ratio's power comes from its velocity component alone.

## Evidence
- 5 non-tautological formulations (L+P excluded from inputs): best R²=0.16 (Re4)
- Simple commits: r=0.608, R²=0.370. Overhead alone: r=0.044. Expert momentum: r=0.005
- Regime separation: turbulent (n=54) produces 2.59x laminar (n=7) at Re_crit=0.6
- Inverted hypothesis: high Re (turbulent) = high output, not low Re (laminar)

## Isomorphism
ISO-FLD1: Reynolds analog operationalized. In fluids, turbulence enables mixing. In swarm, "turbulence" (high velocity) enables knowledge production. Dimensionless normalization preserves classification but loses predictive power vs raw velocity.

## Rule extracted
When building dimensionless ratios, test if the ratio outperforms its strongest individual component. Normalization can add interpretive value while reducing predictive power.
